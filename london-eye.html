London Eye

Europe's Tallest Cantilevered Observation Wheel

Millennium Landmark

The London Eye was conceived and built as part of London's millennium celebrations, opening to the public in March 2000. Originally intended as a temporary attraction with a five-year lease, its immense popularity led to it becoming a permanent fixture on London's skyline.

Designed by architects David Marks and Julia Barfield, the Eye was the world's tallest Ferris wheel until 2006. It has since become one of the most popular paid tourist attractions in the United Kingdom, visited by over 3.75 million people annually.

Engineering Marvel

Standing 135 meters (443 feet) tall, the London Eye is the tallest cantilevered observation wheel in the world. The wheel carries 32 sealed and air-conditioned ovoidal passenger capsules, each representing one of London's boroughs and capable of holding 25 people.

Unlike traditional Ferris wheels, the Eye rotates at 0.9 km/h (0.6 mph) - about 26 cm per second - slow enough for passengers to walk on and off while the wheel is moving. A complete rotation takes approximately 30 minutes, offering breathtaking 360-degree views of London.

Visitor Experience

On a clear day, visibility from the top of the London Eye extends up to 40 kilometers (25 miles). Key landmarks visible include Buckingham Palace, St. Paul's Cathedral, the Shard, and the Houses of Parliament. Each capsule features interactive guides that identify landmarks as you ascend.

The experience includes a 4D cinema pre-show and various ticket options including standard flights, fast-track entry, and premium experiences with champagne. Evening flights offer spectacular views of London's illuminated skyline.
