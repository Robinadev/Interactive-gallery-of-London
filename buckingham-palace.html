Buckingham Palace

Official London Residence of the British Monarch

Royal History

Buckingham Palace became the official royal residence in 1837 when Queen Victoria ascended to the throne. The original building was Buckingham House, built in 1703 for the Duke of Buckingham. It was enlarged over the centuries, with the famous balcony added in 1850.

The palace has witnessed countless historical events, royal weddings, and state occasions. During WWII, the palace was bombed nine times but the royal family refused to leave London, boosting public morale.

Palace Architecture

The palace features 775 rooms, including 19 State rooms, 52 royal and guest bedrooms, and 78 bathrooms. The East Front, which faces The Mall, was added in 1847. The palace's style is predominantly neoclassical, with influences from French design.

The palace's most famous features include the balcony used for royal appearances, the Grand Staircase designed by John Nash, and the magnificent Ballroom which is the largest room in the palace at 36.6m long, 18m wide and 13.5m high.

Visitor Experience

The State Rooms are open to visitors during the summer months (July-October) when the royal family is not in residence. Visitors can explore the magnificent rooms used for official entertaining and state ceremonies, all furnished with treasures from the Royal Collection.

Don't miss the famous Changing of the Guard ceremony that takes place in the palace forecourt. The Royal Mews and Queen's Gallery are open most of the year and offer additional insights into royal life and art collections.
