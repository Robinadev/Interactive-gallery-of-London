Buckingham Palace

Official Residence of the British Monarch

Royal History

Buckingham Palace became the official royal residence in 1837 when Queen Victoria ascended to the throne. The original building was Buckingham House, built in 1703 for the Duke of Buckingham. It was enlarged over the centuries by architects including John Nash and Edward Blore.

The palace has witnessed countless historical events, from royal weddings and state banquets to the famous balcony appearances during national celebrations. During WWII, the palace was bombed nine times but the royal family remained in residence, boosting national morale.

Palace Architecture

The palace features 775 rooms, including 19 State rooms, 52 royal and guest bedrooms, 188 staff bedrooms, 92 offices, and 78 bathrooms. The East Front, which faces The Mall, was added in 1847 and features the famous balcony where the royal family appears on special occasions.

The palace's style is predominantly neoclassical, with influences from French design. The magnificent Ballroom is the largest multi-purpose room in the palace, while the Throne Room features elaborate decor and the original chairs used at the coronation of Queen Elizabeth II.

Visitor Experience

The State Rooms are open to visitors during the summer months (July-October) when the royal family is not in residence. Visitors can explore the magnificent rooms used for official entertaining and state ceremonies, including the White Drawing Room, Music Room, and Ballroom.

The Royal Mews and Queen's Gallery are open most of the year. Don't miss the Changing of the Guard ceremony, which takes place at 11:00 AM on certain days - check the schedule online as it can change due to weather and state events.
